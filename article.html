Shaka iLembe Sweeps the 2024 SAFTAs With a Record-Breaking 12 Awards

The Bomb Shelter's epic historical drama took home twelve South African Film and Television Awards in a single night — the most ever won by a single production in one ceremony. We look back at the journey from script to the stage at Gallagher Estate.

BS
The Bomb Shelter
Editorial, Johannesburg
February 2024
8 min
Awards & Industry
▣ Shaka iLembe Season 1 — Mzansi Magic, 2023 Photograph: The Bomb Shelter Productions

On the evening of the 2024 South African Film and Television Awards, something happened that nobody in the industry had seen before. As the ceremony unfolded at Gallagher Estate in Midrand, a single production — Shaka iLembe, The Bomb Shelter Productions' sweeping historical epic about the rise of the Zulu king — claimed award after award, category after category, until the final tally reached twelve. Twelve. A number that has no precedent in the twenty-year history of the SAFTAs.

It was, by any measure, a reckoning. Not just for the production itself, but for what it represented: the maturation of a South African screen industry capable of delivering world-class period drama; the vindication of a creative vision that took years and considerable courage to bring to fruition; and a statement of intent from a production company that has spent nearly three decades insisting that African stories deserve the grandest possible treatment.

"We always knew this story was worth telling on the biggest scale we could imagine. The industry agreed."

— Angus Gibson, Founder & CEO, The Bomb Shelter Productions

The Making of a Landmark

Shaka iLembe did not happen quickly. The project gestated for the better part of a decade before a single frame was shot. Research teams travelled across KwaZulu-Natal, consulting historians, oral tradition custodians and cultural advisers to ensure that the world being constructed on screen had the weight of authenticity behind it. The script — developed over multiple years — ran to hundreds of pages across two seasons, tracing Shaka's extraordinary journey from an ostracised child to the architect of one of the most formidable military and political organisations southern Africa had ever seen.

The production design alone was a feat of ambition rarely attempted in local television. Costume departments worked from museum archives and historical illustrations to recreate the regalia, weaponry and everyday dress of 19th-century Zulu society. Sets were constructed across multiple locations in KwaZulu-Natal, with the production at its peak employing hundreds of crew members and thousands of extras. The budget was among the largest ever committed to a South African drama series — and every rand of it is visible on screen.

"Twelve SAFTAs in one night. A record that may stand for a generation."

The Awards in Full

When the nominations were announced earlier in 2024, Shaka iLembe led the field by a considerable margin. The categories spanned the breadth of what makes a great production: the writing, the direction, the performances both lead and supporting, the design, the cinematography, the music. On the night, the production converted nominations into wins at an extraordinary rate.

  • Best Drama Series — Shaka iLembe, Season 1
  • Best Director — Drama
  • Best Lead Performance — Male
  • Best Lead Performance — Female
  • Best Supporting Performance — Male
  • Best Supporting Performance — Female
  • Best Original Script
  • Best Cinematography
  • Best Costume Design
  • Best Production Design
  • Best Original Score
  • Best Editing

What It Means for South African Television

Beyond the trophy count, Shaka iLembe's SAFTA sweep carries a meaning that extends well beyond any single production company or production. It signals that South African television — when properly resourced, properly developed and properly ambitious — can compete with the best period drama being made anywhere on earth. It demonstrates that audiences, both domestically and internationally, have an appetite for African historical narratives told with the same scale and craft as anything coming out of the United Kingdom or the United States.

The International Question

Mzansi Magic's decision to commission the series at scale — and the broadcaster's willingness to stand behind a project of this complexity and cost — deserves recognition in its own right. Great content does not emerge without great partnerships, and the broadcaster-producer relationship that delivered Shaka iLembe is a template the industry would do well to study.

The series has already attracted significant international attention, with conversations ongoing about distribution and adaptation rights in multiple territories. The story of Shaka — a military genius, a political revolutionary, a figure of profound complexity — is one that translates across cultures and continents. That it has been told, at last, by South Africans, with South African craft, on South African terms, is something worth celebrating beyond the statistics of a single awards night.

"This belongs to everyone who worked on it. The crew in the mud on a cold KwaZulu-Natal morning. The writers who argued about every scene. The cast who gave everything."

— The Bomb Shelter Productions, SAFTA acceptance remarks, 2024

Looking Forward

Season 2 of Shaka iLembe is in production. The expectations, after a twelve-SAFTA sweep, could not be higher. But The Bomb Shelter has always worked better under pressure — the company was built in the crucible of a country reinventing itself, and it has never been more comfortable than when the stakes are at their highest. The story of Shaka is far from over. And neither, it seems, is The Bomb Shelter's.
